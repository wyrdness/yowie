## Overview

The Yowie is the best-known Australian hairy-hominid cryptid. Aboriginal nations across the continent have long-standing traditions of large, hair-covered bipedal beings under many names — Quinkin and joogabinna in parts of Queensland; Ghindaring, Jurrawarra, Myngawin, Puttikan, Doolaga, Gulaga, and Thoolagal in New South Wales; Pangkarlangu, Jimbra, Tjangara, Wawee, and Noocoonah elsewhere. The English term 'Yowie' itself was first documented in 1875 by Reverend William Ridley as a Gamilaraay word, 'Yō-wī', for 'a spirit that roams the earth at night'. Colonial-era newspapers carried 'indigenous ape' and 'yahoo' reports from the 1850s onward; the most cited 19th-century case is amateur naturalist Henry James McCooey's 1882 description of an ape-like creature on the New South Wales south coast between Batemans Bay and Ulladulla. Modern Yowie research crystallised in the 1970s around the work of Rex Gilroy in the Blue Mountains and was later substantially extended by journalists Tony Healy and Paul Cropper. Witnesses describe a bipedal, hair-covered creature 2.1–3.6 m (7–12 ft) tall with a wide, flat nose and reportedly outsized footprints, though track evidence is highly inconsistent. Reports continue into the 21st century, clustering in Queensland (especially the Springbrook–Gold Coast hinterland), the Blue Mountains and Brindabella Ranges of New South Wales/ACT, and parts of the Northern Territory.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: The English form 'Yowie' is drawn from 'Yō-wī', recorded by Reverend William Ridley in 1875 as a Gamilaraay (northern NSW) word for a night-roaming spirit. Some commentators have proposed parallel influences from the colonial-era 'yahoo' (after Swift's Gulliver's Travels), but the Aboriginal-language attestation is older.
- **Meaning**: Glossed by Ridley as 'a spirit that roams the earth at night'.
- **First use**: 1875 (Ridley, Gamilaraay vocabulary); the related English-language 'Yahoo-Devil Devil' is attested from at least 1876.

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Spirit / Dreaming entity** _(Aboriginal cultural commentators)_

Within Aboriginal frameworks, beings such as the Quinkin, Doolagahl, and Pangkarlangu are spirit entities embedded in country and the Dreaming, not zoological animals.

### Scientific

**Relict / undescribed Australian hominid** _(Rex Gilroy)_

A surviving relict population of an extinct hominid or great ape, possibly related to early Homo, persisting in Australia's most remote forests.

**Misremembered megafauna hypothesis** _(Selected popular cryptozoological writers)_

Some commentators have suggested Yowie traditions may preserve memories of extinct Australian megafauna or unrecognised marsupial species, though this is highly speculative and not supported in mainstream zoology.

### Skeptical

**Misidentification and folklore drift** _(Graham Joyner)_

Yowie reports are most parsimoniously explained by misidentification of known animals (especially in twilight conditions), hoaxes, and the cultural transmission of expectation; the modern 'Yowie' concept is in significant part a 1970s consolidation of older 'yahoo' lore.

**No biological evidence** _(Mainstream Australian zoology)_

Despite a century and a half of reports, no specimen, body, bone, or unambiguous DNA result has been produced; the scientific community treats the Yowie as folklore by default.


## Indigenous Perspectives

> ⚠️ **English-language Yowie writing has often flattened a wide range of different Aboriginal traditions into a single 'Australian Bigfoot'. That move is contested and should be handled carefully.**

**Cultures**: Gamilaraay, Aboriginal nations of Cape York (Quinkin traditions), Aboriginal nations of NSW (Doolagahl, Ghindaring, Jurrawarra, Myngawin, Puttikan, Gulaga, Thoolagal), Aboriginal nations of central and Western Australia (Pangkarlangu, Jimbra, Tjangara, Wawee, Noocoonah)

These beings hold places in Aboriginal cosmologies that are distinct from, and predate, the cryptozoological 'Yowie' category.
